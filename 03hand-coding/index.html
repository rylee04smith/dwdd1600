Dachshund History

Origin

Dachshunds were originally bred in Germany in the 15th century to hunt badgers. The name "Dachshund" translates to "badger dog" in German. They were designed to be long and low to the ground, allowing them to dig into burrows and chase out badgers.

Timeline of Dachshund History

  • 16th 17th Centuries: Dachshunds originated in Germany, bred by foresters and hunters to hunt badgers and other burrow-dwelling animals. Their distinctive long body and short legs were intentionally developed for digging into dens.
  • 18th Century: German breeders began producing Dachshunds consistently, refining their size, temperament, and physical traits to optimize their hunting abilities.
  • Early 1800s: The miniature Dachshund was developed in response to a boom in Germany's rabbit population, creating a smaller version of the breed for hunting smaller prey.
  • 1879: The first formal breed standard for Dachshunds was written in Germany, establishing guidelines for their appearance, temperament, and size.
  • 19th Century: Dachshunds transitioned from working dogs to beloved companions, gaining popularity in both Germany and abroad, particularly in Great Britain.
  • Late 19th Early 20th Century: The breed's popularity continued to grow, and Dachshunds were recognized by kennel clubs in Europe and the United States.
