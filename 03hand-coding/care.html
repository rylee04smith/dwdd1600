Dachshund Care and Health

Care Needs

Exercise: Dachshunds require at least 30 minutes of moderate exercise daily, split into two sessions to avoid overexertion. Interactive games like fetch or tug-of-war help keep them mentally and physically stimulated. Avoid high-impact activities such as jumping off furniture, climbing stairs, or intense running, as these can damage their spine. Weight management is critical, as obesity worsens spinal and joint issues—monitor their diet and exercise closely.

Diet: Portion control is essential, as Dachshunds gain weight easily. Feed them high-quality food rich in protein and low in fillers to support muscle and joint health. Avoid table scraps, fatty foods, and excessive treats.

Grooming: Dachshunds come in three coat types—smooth, long-haired, or wire-haired—each requiring different grooming care. Smooth-coated Dachshunds need weekly brushing and occasional baths, while long-haired varieties require brushing 2 3 times a week to prevent tangles. Wire-haired Dachshunds may need hand-stripping (plucking dead hair) every few months or professional grooming. Trim their nails monthly to prevent discomfort and mobility issues, and clean their ears weekly to avoid infections, especially in floppy-eared varieties.

Health Concerns

  1. Spinal Issues (IVDD): Intervertebral Disc Disease (IVDD) is a common and serious condition in Dachshunds due to their long spines. It occurs when discs between vertebrae degenerate or herniate, causing pain, nerve damage, or even paralysis. To prevent IVDD, avoid stairs and jumping by using ramps or carrying them when moving between furniture and floors. Provide supportive bedding, such as orthopedic or memory foam beds, to reduce spinal stress. Always use a front-clip harness instead of a collar to avoid neck strain. Symptoms of IVDD include reluctance to move, a hunched back, yelping in pain, or loss of bladder/bowel control—this is an emergency, and you should see a vet immediately. Treatment may involve crate rest and anti-inflammatory meds for mild cases, while severe cases may require surgery.

  2. Obesity: Extra weight strains their spine, joints, and heart. Prevent obesity by monitoring food intake, avoiding overfeeding, and ensuring regular, low-impact exercise.
  3. Dental Health: Dachshunds are prone to dental problems due to their small jaws. Prevent issues by brushing their teeth 2–3 times a week and providing dental chews or toys.
  4. Patellar Luxation: This condition involves the kneecap dislocating, causing lameness or arthritis over time. Maintain a healthy weight and avoid excessive jumping to reduce the risk.
  5. Heart Disease: Dachshunds are prone to heart conditions, such as mitral valve disease. Regular vet check-ups, especially as they age, are essential for early detection and management.
  6. Eye Conditions: Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA) and cataracts can affect Dachshunds. Schedule annual eye exams with a veterinary ophthalmologist to monitor and address these issues.
  7. Vet Visits and Preventative Care
    • Annual check-ups are critical for early detection of spinal, heart, and dental issues.
    • Keep vaccinations up-to-date and discuss non-core vaccines, such as leptospirosis, with your vet.
    • Regular flea, tick, and heartworm prevention is also important, especially if your Dachshund spends time outdoors.
  8. Special Considerations for Aging Dachshunds
    • As Dachshunds age (around 7–8 years), monitor for arthritis, decreased mobility, and weight gain.
    • Provide adaptations such as ramps, orthopedic beds, and softer food if dental issues arise.
  9. When to See a Vet Immediately
    • Seek immediate veterinary care if you notice signs of IVDD, such as sudden back pain, inability to walk, or loss of bladder control.
    • Other red flags include severe lethargy, loss of appetite, difficulty breathing, persistent coughing, limping, or reluctance to move, as these could indicate patellar luxation or arthritis.
